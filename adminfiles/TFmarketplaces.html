03Drop-Ship by Brand Region

Platform infrastructure evaluation — which marketplace tech can onboard brands and process payouts for sellers based in APAC (China · Japan · Korea) and Western markets (EU · UK · US). Not TF product features.
Model: TF does not hold stock. Brands from any region — EU, UK, US, China, Japan, Korea — list on TF and ship directly to the end customer. TF provides the purchasing infrastructure, collects payment, routes the order to the brand, and takes a commission — comparable to Amazon's third-party marketplace model.

Two compliance issues apply regardless of platform choice:

1. EU + UK marketplace VAT liability (IOSS / deemed supplier rule): Under EU VAT rules in force since July 2021, when a marketplace facilitates the sale of goods shipped from outside the EU to an EU buyer (value ≤ €150), the marketplace — not the brand — is the deemed supplier responsible for collecting and remitting VAT. The UK has an equivalent rule for goods ≤ £135 (in force since January 2021). TF would need EU IOSS registration and a UK VAT number, plus an integrated tax remittance layer. No platform in this table handles this natively; a third-party service (e.g. Avalara, Taxually) is required on top of any platform.

2. US de minimis for Chinese-origin goods: As of 2025, the $800 de minimis exemption has been eliminated for goods of Chinese origin shipped to the US. Each package from a Chinese brand to a US customer requires a formal customs entry with applicable duties — a per-shipment compliance burden independent of platform. Japanese and Korean goods are not affected; the standard $800 de minimis threshold still applies.

04Cross-Border Compliance: All Origin–Destination Routes

Marketplace vs affiliate by region. TF can operate as a marketplace (owns the transaction, collects payment, handles tax, pays co-creator revenue shares automatically) or as an affiliate network (links out to brand sites; brand handles checkout and compliance; TF earns a tracked commission). Domestic routes — US→US, EU→EU — suit the marketplace model: compliance is manageable and TF must own the transaction to pay co-creator rewards without relying on brand-reported figures. Cross-border routes — EU→UK, US→EU, APAC→West — add IOSS, UK VAT, FTA origin documentation, and customs duties; an affiliate model eliminates TF's compliance burden on these routes but forfeits transaction control and makes automated co-creator payouts unauditable.
MVP Market: Start Domestic

For MVP, prioritise domestic routes: US brands → US consumers and EU brands → EU consumers. Both eliminate IOSS, UK deemed-supplier VAT, FTA origin documentation, and cross-border customs duties. Compliance reduces to US state sales tax (automated via Shopify Tax) and EU domestic VAT (OSS, handled natively by most platforms). Cross-border routes — EU → UK, EU → US, US → EU — can be added post-traction; the DDP infrastructure (Global-e / Zonos + Avalara IOSS) is proven at scale and ready, but adds operational overhead not justified before product–market fit is established.
Compliance rules that apply regardless of origin or platform:

1. EU IOSS — marketplace deemed supplier (in force July 2021): When TF facilitates a sale of goods shipped from outside the EU to an EU buyer where the consignment value is ≤ €150, TF — not the brand — is the deemed supplier under EU VAT rules and must collect VAT at the point of sale and remit it via EU IOSS registration. Goods >€150 clear customs normally (import VAT + duties paid by buyer at border). One IOSS number covers all 27 EU member states. ec.europa.eu — IOSS

2. UK equivalent deemed supplier rule (in force January 2021): Identical logic for goods ≤ £135 shipped from outside the UK to a UK buyer. TF is responsible for UK VAT collection and remittance. A separate UK VAT number is required — IOSS covers EU only, not UK. gov.uk — overseas goods on online marketplaces

3. US de minimis elimination for Chinese-origin goods (2025): The $800 de minimis exemption has been eliminated for goods of Chinese origin. Every parcel from a Chinese brand to a US customer requires a formal customs entry (ISF filing, HS codes, Section 301 and executive order duties) regardless of value. Japan- and Korea-origin goods retain the standard $800 de minimis. ustr.gov — Section 301

4. China CBEC (Cross-Border E-Commerce) regime: Foreign goods sold online to Chinese consumers must comply with China's CBEC framework: GACC registration, product listing on the China Customs CBEC system, and integrated CBEC tax (approximately 9.1% for most fashion goods). No standard Western marketplace platform handles this natively. Requires a specialist CBEC provider or listing on existing China cross-border platforms (Tmall Global, JD Worldwide, Kaola). This barrier applies regardless of whether the brand's origin is EU, UK, US, Korea, or Japan.

Legend — abbreviations used above
HS code — Harmonized System product classification code (6-digit international standard used by customs authorities worldwide to identify goods and determine tariff rates)
ISF — Importer Security Filing ("10+2 filing") required by US CBP for ocean freight shipments entering the US; must be filed 24 hours before vessel loading
IOSS — Import One-Stop Shop (EU scheme since July 2021): marketplace registers once and remits EU VAT for all 27 member states on goods ≤€150 sold to EU buyers from outside the EU
OSS — One-Stop Shop (EU VAT simplification for EU-based sellers on cross-border B2C sales within the EU — distinct from IOSS which is for imports)
FTA — Free Trade Agreement: treaty reducing or eliminating customs tariffs between signatory countries; origin proof required to claim preferential rates
TCA — EU-UK Trade and Cooperation Agreement (post-Brexit, in force January 2021): zero tariffs on goods meeting rules of origin between EU and UK
EPA — Economic Partnership Agreement; specifically used here for the EU-Japan EPA (in force February 2019): reduces/eliminates EU-Japan tariffs on most goods
CEPA — Comprehensive Economic Partnership Agreement (UK-Japan, in force January 2021): tariff reductions broadly matching EU-Japan EPA
KORUS — Korea-US Free Trade Agreement (in force March 2012): zero or near-zero tariffs on most goods traded between South Korea and the US (bilateral)
RCEP — Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (in force January 2022): multilateral FTA covering ASEAN nations + China, Japan, South Korea, Australia, New Zealand; provides tariff reductions across member pairs
MFN — Most Favoured Nation rate: the standard WTO tariff applied without any preferential FTA; the default rate when no trade agreement is in force between two countries
DDP — Delivered Duty Paid (Incoterm): seller or platform collects all duties and taxes at checkout; logistics provider handles customs clearance; buyer pays one all-inclusive landed cost
DDU — Delivered Duty Unpaid (Incoterm): buyer is responsible for paying duties at the destination border; risk of surprise charges and delivery refusal
De minimis — minimum consignment value threshold below which customs duties are waived (US: $800 for most origins, eliminated for Chinese-origin goods; EU: €150 customs + VAT threshold; UK: £135 customs duty threshold)
CBEC — Cross-Border E-Commerce: China's regulatory framework for foreign goods sold online to Chinese consumers, administered through GACC; requires seller registration, product listing, and integrated tax payment
GACC — General Administration of Customs of China: regulates all goods entering China including CBEC; foreign sellers must register brand, products, and platform with GACC before first sale
JCT — Japan Consumption Tax (10%): applies to goods imported into Japan; on standard B2C small-parcel imports, paid by the buyer at customs clearance
EUR.1 — Movement certificate used to prove origin under EU FTAs and the EU-UK TCA; issued by customs authority of the exporting country; required to claim preferential tariff rates at destination
Global-e — Cross-border DDP SaaS platform (Nasdaq: GLBE): integrates with Shopify and other commerce stacks to offer localised checkout — local currency, local payment methods, duties + VAT collected at point of sale and remitted to authorities
Zonos — Cross-border landed cost and customs duty calculation platform; Shopify native integration; real-time duty + tax estimate at checkout; supports DDP model
Avalara — Tax automation SaaS: covers US state sales tax, EU VAT / IOSS, UK VAT, and cross-border duty calculation; integrates with most commerce platforms in this comparison
Conclusion

EU and UK VAT always falls on TF regardless of origin — both markets apply the deemed-supplier rule, requiring IOSS (EU) and a separate UK VAT number. No platform in this comparison handles these natively; Avalara or Taxually is required on top of any stack, with Zonos or Global-e for DDP landed cost at checkout.

China as a destination is blocked for any standard Western marketplace platform. The CBEC regime (GACC registration, integrated tax, CNY payment) requires a specialist provider regardless of where the brand is located.

Korea and Japan are the least complex origins across all three destination markets: both have active FTAs with the EU and UK, Korea has KORUS with the US, and both retain the $800 US de minimis. US → EU is the proven route — the DDP infrastructure (Global-e, Zonos + Avalara IOSS) is in production at major US fashion brands today.

05MVP Strategy

Domestic routes only at MVP — US→US and EU→EU. No customs, no import VAT, no FTA documentation. US state sales tax must be collected and remitted — requires a tax API integration (see below). EU domestic VAT is the brand's own obligation as an EU registrant. This is the only route where TF can ship without cross-border compliance infrastructure.
Custom-built drop pages — compliance stack at MVP (domestic only)

Payment + tax: Stripe + Stripe Tax. Stripe Tax is an API add-on to the standard Stripe integration — no separate tax service required at MVP. It handles US state sales tax (economic nexus tracking, automated calculation and collection across all states), EU domestic VAT (OSS-aware for intra-EU cross-border sales), and UK VAT. One line of configuration in the Stripe dashboard activates it on any custom checkout. stripe.com/tax

Marketplace payouts: Stripe Connect. Splits each transaction automatically — brand receives their share minus TF commission, co-creator revenue shares can be routed as separate transfers. No manual payout calculation. Works with any custom frontend via API. stripe.com/connect

EU OSS (One Stop Shop). If TF facilitates sales from EU-based brands to consumers in other EU member states (e.g. French brand → German consumer), TF may need EU OSS registration — one registration covers all 27 member states for intra-EU cross-border sales. Free; filed quarterly. Different from IOSS (IOSS applies only to imports from outside the EU). Verify with EU tax counsel whether TF or the brand is the deemed supplier for intra-EU sales on a custom platform. ec.europa.eu — OSS

What TF must build itself — product pages, checkout flow, order management, brand dashboard, shipping label generation, return handling. No platform provides this out of the box on a custom stack. Stripe handles money movement and tax; everything else is custom code.
Cross-border cannot be done "by hand" without platform compliance. The EU IOSS deemed-supplier rule and the UK equivalent attach to TF the moment TF collects payment — regardless of whether orders are processed on a platform or manually. TF is the deemed supplier in EU/UK law; the liability does not pass to the brand.

Without IOSS registration: every sub-€150 cross-border parcel shipped to the EU hits customs, the consumer is charged import VAT at delivery, and TF still owes the VAT it failed to collect. Tax authorities can audit and demand back-payment plus penalties for every unprocessed order — accruing silently while TF ships.

China-origin goods shipped to the US: the $800 de minimis exemption was eliminated in 2025. Every parcel requires a formal customs entry (ISF filing, HS codes, applicable duties) regardless of value. Brands cannot simply ship a package.
Two clean options for cross-border post-MVP:

1. Build marketplace compliance — EU IOSS registration (~2–4 weeks, free) + UK VAT number (up to 40 business days, free) + Avalara or Taxually for automated remittance + Zonos or Global-e for DDP landed cost at checkout. This is the proven path used by major US fashion brands today. TF owns the transaction and can pay co-creator revenue shares automatically.

2. Affiliate model for cross-border routes — consumer clicks out to the brand's own site; brand handles checkout, VAT, and shipping. TF earns a tracked commission. Zero compliance burden for TF on these routes — but TF loses transaction control, order data, and the ability to calculate and pay co-creator revenue shares without relying on brand-reported figures.
