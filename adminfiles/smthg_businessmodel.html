The Circles Model

Community-gated drops, not a distribution pipe. Access is gated by referrals, not by follower count — that is the difference between a drop that sells once and a drop that spreads.

Pipe model — does not spread

Designer → Influencer → Virtual try-on → Consumer buys

One-way funnel. Scarcity exists, but nobody has a reason to bring the next person in. Growth depends entirely on how many people the influencer already reaches.

Circles model — has a loop

Circle → Drop → Deposit → Referral unlock → New member

Each buyer has a concrete reason to invite the next: earlier access to the next drop. Growth compounds through the community, not just through reach.

Five components

01

Circle (community layer)

Per-designer group: chat, a content library (lookbooks, styling, behind-the-scenes), and live designer events. Free to join — community forms before any purchase.

Structural pattern: cohort/community platforms (e.g. Skool)
02

Real scarcity

Small batch, true visible remaining count. Never inflated — fabricated urgency is a legal and trust risk, not just a marketing one.

Modeled on Patta drops · caution from Booking.com's CMA warning
03

Commitment device

Pre-order deposit to hold a spot in the drop. No deposit, no spot — kills flaking and signals real demand to the designer before production.

Modeled on Breeze — pay-upfront per date, 600,000+ dates organized
04

Referral unlock

Inviting someone into the Circle moves you up the queue or unlocks earlier access to the next drop. This is the actual growth loop — not a link post, a gated reason to invite.

Modeled on Robinhood's referral waitlist — 1M users pre-launch, ~3 signups per referrer
05

Ambassador = Circle host

Ambassadors run their own Circle and host events rather than just posting a link. Revenue share pays out on what their Circle actually converts.

Existing economics: 10% GMV commission, 10–20% ambassador reward (tiered)

Loop repeats at the next drop — each cycle recruits the next member instead of just selling to the last one.

What breaks this model

  • Fabricating the remaining count or the countdown — Booking.com was found to misstate "only 1 room left" in spot-checks and now faces an €8B class action.
  • Anchoring the mechanic to a temporary tailwind instead of a permanent pain — Clubhouse's invite-only scarcity hit $4B in a year, then collapsed once lockdowns ended; Groupon's urgency deals couldn't sustain past their novelty phase; Peloton's growth reversed once its pandemic tailwind faded.
