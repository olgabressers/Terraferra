The Circles Model

What SMTHG stands for

For Shoppers
Limited drops. Limited windows.
Do not miss.
Fashion you can't find anywhere else.
Discover. Try on. Make it happen.
Join live events with the designer.
Chat and swap looks with people who share your style.
Membership levels that unlock perks from designers and the platform.
For Designers
Release your drop.
Produce what sells.
Your visual identity. Your community. Zero leftover stock.
Built for designers who won't produce without demand.
Hold live sessions — Q&A, styling, behind-the-scenes — with your own community.
See what your community actually wants before you produce.
Reward repeat members with status that carries drop after drop.

Pipe model — does not spread

Designer → Influencer → Virtual try-on → Consumer buys

One-way funnel. Scarcity exists, but nobody has a reason to bring the next person in. Growth depends entirely on how many people the influencer already reaches.

Circles model — has a loop

Circle → Drop → Deposit → Referral unlock → New member

Each buyer has a concrete reason to invite the next: earlier access to the next drop. Growth compounds through the community, not just through reach.

Five components

01

Circle (community layer)

Per-designer group, free to join — community forms before any purchase, not just at checkout. What's inside, below.

Structural pattern: cohort/community platforms (e.g. Skool)
02

Real scarcity

Small batch, true visible remaining count. Never inflated — fabricated urgency is a legal and trust risk, not just a marketing one.

Modeled on Patta drops · caution from Booking.com's CMA warning
03

Commitment device

Pre-order deposit to hold a spot in the drop. No deposit, no spot — kills flaking and signals real demand to the designer before production.

Modeled on Breeze — pay-upfront per date, 600,000+ dates organized
04

Referral unlock

Inviting someone into the Circle moves you up the queue or unlocks earlier access to the next drop. This is the actual growth loop — not a link post, a gated reason to invite.

Modeled on Robinhood's referral waitlist — 1M users pre-launch, ~3 signups per referrer
05

Influencer = Circle host

Influencers run their own Circle and host events rather than just posting a link. Revenue share pays out on what their Circle actually converts.

Existing economics: 10% GMV commission, 10–20% influencer reward (tiered)

The pain this closes — per role

Designers / Brands
Inventory before demand

Producing stock upfront is expensive, and marketplaces then take 25–52% of every sale.

Pay thousands before selling anything

Existing platforms charge thousands a year upfront and require stock already in place.

No relationship with buyers after the sale

Once a sale closes, the designer has no direct line to those buyers — community gives them live sessions and repeat members instead of a one-off transaction.

No feedback before the next collection

Designers decide what to make next without input from the people who actually buy — community gives them a direct read from their own audience.

No recurring buyers

Every drop starts from zero — no base of superfans who buy again and again.

Consumers
Can't find their own style

No fashion-native place to discover emerging brands or a personal look — just the same mass-market trends.

Wearing what everyone wears

Mass-market supply means nothing bought is theirs alone.

Nowhere to belong between drops

Once a drop ends, nothing brings them back until the next one starts from zero — no shared space to discuss it, revisit it, or connect with others who bought the same drop.

Discovery is solitary

Finding an emerging designer happens alone, scrolling — there's no group of people who found it too.

No one to discuss an outfit with

Deciding on a look or asking for style advice happens alone — no group of people with similar taste to ask.

Influencers
Locked out by minimums

89.5% of influencers — the nano and micro tiers under 50K whose communities actually buy — don't fit existing platforms.

Nothing between campaigns

Creator work is one-off deals — after a campaign ends, nothing brings them back until the next negotiation starts from zero. Hosting a Circle gives them an ongoing reason to stay engaged, not just a per-drop payout.

No stable income

One-off deals mean starting the pitch over every time — a long-term Circle with the same designer gives them a recurring, predictable income instead.

Can't cross-sell their own audience

Working with several designers of a similar style today means separate, disconnected deals — a shared Circle audience lets them sell across all of them at once.

Booking.com — earliest years, not a revenue chart

No dollar figures are publicly available for 1996–2008 (Booking.com/Bookings.nl was private, then part of Priceline's international segment without a clean standalone breakout) — so this is a timeline, not a bar chart, to avoid implying precision that doesn't exist. Launched Nov 1996 as Bookings.nl, Amsterdam, grew slowly through hotel partnerships. 2003 — Google AdWords launches; Bookings.nl becomes one of Google's largest advertisers almost overnight, "the moment the business really started flying." 2005 — acquired by Priceline for $133M (acquisition price, not revenue). Priceline Group's international segment (largely Booking.com) then grew into the multi-billion-dollar business it is today.
