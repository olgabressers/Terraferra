Designers, Investors, Co-owners & Employees — Russian Residents · US Sanctions
Research as of June 2026.
Direct answers
Who Allowed? Key limitations
Designers & brands based in Russia YES
1. Payouts require a non-Russian bank account — card payments and transfers to Russia blocked since 2022. Infrastructure issue, not a legal ban on TF.
2. Do not provide strategic business advice to Russia-based users. US law prohibits "management consulting" to persons in Russia — AI features giving growth strategy, pricing advice, competitive analysis are covered. Sales dashboards, product search, discovery are not.
3. Must not be on the US Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) blacklist. Stripe checks this automatically at payout.
Investors based in Russia YES
1. Must not be on the SDN blacklist.
2. Transferring money from Russia to TF is their logistical problem — most Russia-to-West transfers blocked. Not TF's legal issue.
3. Paying dividends: legally fine, but they need a non-Russian bank account to receive it.
Co-founders & employees based in Russia YES
1. Holding equity: yes, if not on the SDN blacklist.
2. Paying salary: no US sanctions law prohibits paying a Russia-resident person working for TF. EO 14071 prohibits TF providing services to Russia — the direction is opposite. Real constraint: US banks often refuse Russia wire transfers. SDN screening required before payment.
3. Practical path: pay to a Georgian, Armenian, or EU bank account. No sanctions barrier — infrastructure only.

Legal requirements — must do
1.Use a regulated payment processor — it handles SDN screening automatically. OFAC prohibits transacting with SDN-listed persons (strict liability — penalty applies even without knowledge). In practice, this obligation is met by using Stripe or equivalent: Stripe automatically screens all connected accounts against the OFAC SDN list and pauses payouts to any flagged account. No user-facing nationality declaration is required or expected. Stripe sanctions support ↗ OFAC SDN FAQ ↗
2.Do not actively market AI advisory or strategic consulting features to Russia-located users. Two prohibitions apply where TF has knowledge the user is in Russia: (a) "management consulting" ban — strategic recommendations, growth strategy, competitive analysis; (b) since September 2024, cloud-delivered BI and CRM software where TF knowingly provides it to Russia-based users. Standard browsing, product discovery, and social features are outside both. Compliance approach used by all major platforms: geoblock Russia for advanced advisory/analytics features; let standard marketplace features operate globally. No platform asks users for passports. OFAC FAQ 1064 ↗ FAQ 1187 ↗
3.Verify beneficial ownership for any legal entity counterparty. Under OFAC's 50% rule, a company is blocked if 50% or more of it is owned in aggregate by one or more blocked persons — two SDN-listed individuals each owning 25% = company blocked. Applies to indirect ownership chains too. OFAC FAQ 399 ↗
01Applicable Sanctions Programs
No blanket ban on fashion. Russian individuals and fashion brands are not restricted by default. Only specific named persons on the Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) list and companies in designated sectors (finance, defense, metals, energy) are blocked. Fashion is not a designated sector.
Program Scope Covers TF fashion marketplace?
EO 14024 (31 CFR §587) Blocks designated individuals and entities in named sectors: financial services, technology, defense, aerospace, electronics, metals, energy, manufacturing, management consulting, architecture, engineering. OFAC EO 14024 program page ↗ Fashion not designated. Non-designated Russian individuals are not blocked.
EO 14071 — Prohibited Services US persons cannot provide: accounting, management consulting, architecture, engineering, trust/corporate formation, IT consultancy, IT support and cloud-based services for enterprise management software and design/manufacturing software to persons in Russia. OFAC FAQ 1128 ↗ Consumer marketplace = outside scope. Management consulting / brand advisory features = legal risk (see §03).
EO 13685 (Crimea) Imports from / exports to / investments in Crimea. Not relevant to a digital platform with no Crimea-specific nexus.
SSI List (Sectoral Sanctions) Debt / equity transactions with major Russian financial and energy companies on the SSI list. Not relevant. TF is not providing debt, equity, or financing to sanctioned entities.
GL 25G (Russia internet services) General License authorizing internet communications services to Russia: browsing, messaging, social features, web hosting, user authentication. Does not authorize commercial transactions or payments. Superseded GL 25F effective October 30, 2024. Federal Register — GL 25G (Oct 2024) ↗ Covers TF's platform access layer (browsing, social). Does NOT cover the transaction layer — Russian users buying goods or money moving through the platform requires separate analysis.
02IT Services Prohibition — Does It Apply?
Marketplace features are outside scope. Buying, selling, product discovery, pre-orders, and payments are not enterprise management or manufacturing software. One area to watch: do not specifically market analytics dashboards as a Russia-targeted feature — geoblock advanced analytics for Russian IPs if practical.

EO 14071 (effective September 12, 2024) prohibits US persons from providing cloud-based services to Russia for enterprise management software (ERP, CRM, business intelligence, supply chain, project management, data warehousing) and design/manufacturing software (CAD, CAM, BIM, engineer-to-order tools). OFAC FAQs 1184 & 1188 ↗

03SDN Screening
Stripe handles this automatically. No manual passport check or user declaration needed for platform users — Stripe screens every connected account against the SDN list before each payout and blocks matches. For investors and co-founders, run a manual SDN check before signing any legal agreement.

OFAC sanctions are strict liability — liability attaches even without knowledge that a counterparty is on the list. In practice, no platform asks users for their passport or manually checks SDN at signup. Compliance happens through payment processors: Stripe automatically screens all connected accounts against the SDN list at payout and blocks any match. TF does not need a user-facing nationality declaration or manual SDN lookup for individual platform users. Higher manual due diligence applies to business partners (investors, co-founders, large contract counterparties) — not individual designers or influencers. Penalty varies by knowledge: unknowing violations with documented compliance + voluntary disclosure are treated as non-egregious. OFAC FAQ 65 ↗

04Payment Infrastructure
All direct payments to Russia are blocked by infrastructure — not by sanctions law. Solution: require designers and co-founders to provide a non-Russian bank account (Georgian, Armenian, or EU). Georgian and Armenian accounts are widely held by Russian professionals since 2022 and work with Stripe and Wise.
Route Works? Notes
Visa / Mastercard to Russian-issued cards Blocked Both networks suspended Russia operations March 2022. Chip certificates for existing Russian-issued cards expired January 2025. Non-functional. Visa press release ↗
USD wire to Russian bank account (SWIFT) Blocked OFAC has sanctioned Russia's major banks (VTB, Sberbank, Gazprombank). USD transfers to these banks are prohibited for US persons — the barrier is sanctions law, not SWIFT disconnection. SWIFT has formally disconnected ~70 of ~300 Russian member banks; the sanctioned ones are unreachable for US-origin USD payments. EU Council — SWIFT disconnections ↗
Wise / Payoneer (to Russia) Blocked Both suspended Russia-destination transfers. Not available as a Russia-bound payout route.
Stripe → non-Russian bank account (Georgian, Armenian, EU) Works If the Russia-based designer has a bank account outside Russia, Stripe can pay to it. Legally clean. Technically functional. No sanctions issue if the individual is not SDN-listed.
Wise / Payoneer → non-Russian account Works Both work for transfers to Georgian, Armenian, Kazakh, or EU accounts. Used widely by Russian freelancers and contractors since 2022.
Crypto (USDT/USDC stablecoin) Possible but complex Not prohibited for non-SDN individuals, but TF would need to implement AML/KYC procedures for crypto disbursements and manage the legal and compliance overhead. Not a recommended first path.
How it works in practice — the workaround

The solution used by hundreds of thousands of Russian freelancers and contractors since 2022: open a bank account in Georgia, Armenia, or another non-sanctioned country and receive payments there.

For Russia-based designers receiving payouts from TF: TF pays to their Georgian or Armenian account via Stripe or Wise. The designer withdraws locally or converts to rubles via their own bank. No sanctions issue on TF's end — the account is outside Russia, the bank is unsanctioned, and Stripe's automatic SDN check clears at payout.

For Russian consumers buying goods: they cannot pay with a Russian-issued Visa/Mastercard. Options that work: (1) a Georgian or Armenian-issued Visa/Mastercard, which a growing share of Russia-resident buyers already hold; (2) Apple Pay / Google Pay linked to a non-Russian card; (3) MIR card — Russia's domestic network, not accepted by Western processors. In practice, TF's Russia-based consumer base will be those who already have a foreign-issued card or foreign account — a self-selecting group that is already operating across the payment barrier.

Practical requirement TF should add to onboarding: designers must provide a non-Russian bank account to receive payouts. This is standard across all platforms serving Russian creators post-2022 — it is not unusual or burdensome; most Russian professionals in the digital economy already have a Georgian or Armenian account.

Three risks to monitor — none are immediate blockers. (1) AI advisory or strategy features for Russia-located users need a legal opinion before launch; (2) sanctions lists change — build a quarterly review; (3) legal entity counterparties need beneficial ownership checked (SDN catches people, not always the companies they control).

07SDN Screening — Technology & Integration
Stripe covers this automatically at payout. For onboarding, Persona (identity verification + SDN in one flow) is the practical choice for a fashion marketplace. No custom engineering needed — both are API integrations.

The SDN list is a machine-readable database maintained by OFAC. It can be integrated directly into a platform's onboarding and payout flows.

Consumers in Russia Browsing, discovery, and social features are covered by GL 25G. Platform access is not restricted. Paying from Russia: Visa/Mastercard non-functional to Russian-issued cards since 2022. Russian consumers cannot pay via standard card rails. No legal prohibition on accepting payment from a non-SDN Russian consumer — but the payment infrastructure does not support it. Practical conclusion
Russia-based consumers Can browse and engage with the platform. Cannot realistically complete a purchase: card payment is blocked and physical goods cannot be shipped to Russia. No action required to block them — the infrastructure gap prevents transactions automatically. SDN screening is still required if they attempt a purchase.
Russia-based influencers Can participate in content, social, and ambassador activities. Cannot receive commission payouts to a Russian account. Require a non-Russian bank account as a condition of payout — same rule as designers.
Digital-only goods (no physical shipment) If TF were ever to sell digital products (gift cards, tokens, digital downloads) to Russia-based users: no BIS restriction applies to digital goods. GL 25F covers digital content distribution. Payment remains the bottleneck.
09Russian Co-Founders, Investors & Shareholders
Allowed. A Russian national can hold equity, invest via SAFE, receive salary, and receive dividends — as long as they are not on the OFAC SDN blacklist. SDN screening is the single legal requirement. Payment to Russia is blocked by infrastructure (not law) — require a non-Russian bank account.

The determining factor for all ownership instruments — common shares, preferred shares, SAFE, convertible note — is whether the individual is on the OFAC SDN list. The instrument type does not change the rule. SDN-listed: blocked in all forms. Not SDN-listed: permitted in all forms.

EO 14071 "new investment" prohibition runs one direction only: it restricts US persons from investing in Russia. It does not restrict a Russian national from investing in a US company. However, EO 14024 and the SDN blocking regime apply in both directions: receiving funds from any SDN-listed person is prohibited regardless of which direction money flows. In June 2025, OFAC imposed a $215.9M penalty on GVA Capital (a San Francisco VC) for managing investments on behalf of a sanctioned Russian oligarch. Every Russian investor must be screened against the SDN list before any transaction closes. GVA Capital enforcement case ↗

Situation Legal position What this means for TF
Russian national holds any ownership interest (shares, SAFE, note) — not on SDN list Permitted. No provision of US sanctions law prohibits a non-designated Russian national from holding an ownership interest in a US company, in any instrument form. OFAC EO 14024 program page ↗ Screen the individual against the SDN list at the time of grant or investment, and periodically thereafter. If they remain off the list, no restriction applies.
Russian national holds any ownership interest (shares, SAFE, note) — on SDN list Blocked. Paying dividends, distributions, or any other benefit to an SDN-listed person is prohibited. If SDN-listed persons hold 50% or more of TF in aggregate, TF itself becomes a blocked entity and any US person transacting with TF is also in violation. OFAC 50% rule ↗ No SDN-listed person can hold any form of ownership interest in TF. Existing interests must be restructured out before TF can operate legally.
Paying salary or consulting fees to a Russia-resident co-founder Permitted. No US sanctions law prohibits paying a Russia-resident person who works for TF. EO 14071 prohibits TF from providing consulting services to persons in Russia — the direction is opposite. A Russia-resident co-founder providing management services to a US company is the provider, not the recipient. EO 14071 does not apply. OFAC FAQ 1061 — employment by Russian entities ↗ Real constraints: (1) US banks may refuse wire transfers to Russia-resident individuals — practical, not legal. (2) SDN screening required before any payment. (3) Pay to a non-Russian bank account (Georgian, Armenian, EU). No sanctions barrier — infrastructure only.
Paying dividends or profit distributions to a Russia-resident shareholder (non-SDN) Permitted legally, infrastructure blocked. No US sanctions law prohibits distributing dividends to a non-SDN Russian-resident shareholder. The legal right to pay exists. Executing the payment does not: Visa/Mastercard and most SWIFT routes to Russia are non-functional since 2022. Shareholder agreement should require payment to a non-Russian bank account. This is a contractual requirement, not a sanctions requirement.
Required actions
Screen all co-founders and investors against the SDN list At equity grant or investment, at each subsequent funding round, and when OFAC updates the list. Applies to every shareholder regardless of nationality.
Apply the 50% rule to the cap table If SDN-listed persons hold 50% or more of TF equity in aggregate — even if no single person reaches 50% alone — TF itself becomes a blocked entity. Structure the cap table to prevent this outcome. OFAC FAQ 399 ↗
Pay Russia-resident co-founders to a non-Russian bank account No sanctions law bars the payment — the constraint is payment infrastructure. Arrange salary/fees to a Georgian, Armenian, or EU account. SDN screen the individual before every payment.
10Russian Nationality vs. Russian Residency
Conclusion: Sanctions restrictions apply based on location in Russia and SDN-list status — not on Russian passport or nationality. A Russian national living outside Russia is not "a person located in the Russian Federation" for EO 14071 purposes. OFAC FAQ 1058 ↗

11Physical Goods: What Can Be Sold & Shipped to Russian Buyers?
Russian designers selling to US/global buyers: no BIS restriction. BIS controls US exports — goods shipped OUT of Russia by a Russian designer are not a US export. No US license required. Payment from TF to the designer is the only constraint (see §04 for the workaround).
Shipping goods INTO Russia (Russian buyers purchasing): restricted at $300+ wholesale and all art. Fashion apparel below $300/unit wholesale from US-based designers = not restricted by BIS. At or above $300 = blocked. Art objects at any price = blocked. Plus: payment from Russian buyers is blocked by infrastructure regardless. TF's terms of service should prohibit designers from shipping restricted goods to sanctioned countries — this protects TF from BIS intermediary liability. Federal Register — BIS rule ↗

Governed by the US Export Administration Regulations (EAR), enforced by BIS (Bureau of Industry and Security, US Department of Commerce) — separate from OFAC. BIS restricts physical export of "luxury goods" to Russia under Supplement No. 5 to Part 746 of the EAR. The threshold is $300 per unit wholesale price. Items at or above this require an export license, issued under a policy of denial for Russia.

Platform marketplace services (TF's own service) Not covered by BIS N/A TF's platform service is not a physical export. Covered by OFAC rules in §01–02 above. The BIS rule affects the physical goods that designers ship to Russian buyers — not TF's platform itself.
