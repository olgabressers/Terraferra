Tone of Voice

Voice and language principles for consumer-facing copy — app, drop pages, ambassador messaging, community and social captions. Audience: Gen Z and young Millennial fashion shoppers in the US and Europe, the demographic the platform's target designers already sell to.

Recommended tone of voice for SMTHG
Casual but substantive, warm rather than sarcastic, mostly matter-of-fact with enthusiasm reserved for real moments (a drop opening, a reward unlocking) — not scripted into every sentence. Humor is optional, not required.

Avoid

"OMG this drop is literally so unhinged, no cap 🔥🔥🔥"

Slang borrowed from a subculture the platform has no ownership of. Reads as try-hard and dates within a season. Contradicted by Section 03's data — humor and cleverness rank lowest of all measured traits.

Prefer

"72 hours. When it's gone, it's gone — no restock."

Direct, specific, matter-of-fact urgency. States the mechanic instead of performing excitement about it — matches what the data shows this audience rates highest: honesty and consistency.

Avoid

"An exclusive bespoke collection curated for our most valued clients."

Luxury-house vocabulary this segment does not use — see Section 02. Signals a price bracket and formality the target designers do not sit in.

Prefer

"12 pieces, hand-finished by [designer]. First on the list gets first pick."

Elevated through specific, verifiable detail — piece count, maker, process — instead of luxury jargon. Matches the accessible-luxury pattern in Section 02.

01 — Framework: four independent dimensions, not one adjective
Tone is not a single style choice. It is four separate sliders — funny↔serious, formal↔casual, respectful↔irreverent, enthusiastic↔matter-of-fact — that can each sit anywhere on their own scale. Source: Nielsen Norman Group, "The Four Dimensions of Tone of Voice"

Funny ↔ Serious

Whether the copy is trying to be humorous or approaches the subject plainly.

Formal ↔ Casual

Sentence structure, contractions, and how close the writing is to spoken language.

Respectful ↔ Irreverent

Whether the brand treats the reader and the subject with deference or takes liberties.

Enthusiastic ↔ Matter-of-fact

Energy level and emotional intensity — exclamation vs. plain statement.

Nielsen Norman Group, nngroup.com
02 — Where accessible luxury sits between mass and prestige
The target designers sit in the "accessible luxury" segment — priced and positioned between mass market and true luxury, selling on quality, meaning and authenticity rather than exclusivity through price. Comparable brands in this segment use elevated but plain-spoken language, not luxury-house jargon. Source: "Masstige" — Wikipedia, summarizing Michael Silverstein & Neil Fiske, "Luxury for the Masses" (Harvard Business Review) and Trading Up, 2003

True luxury vocabulary reads differently on purpose — "words like 'bespoke,' 'exquisite,' 'heritage,' and 'crafted' evoke a sense of craftsmanship and exclusivity," used consistently across every touchpoint to signal a luxury house. Source: LUXEO, "Mastering the Language of Luxury Branding", 12 Oct 2024.

Practical read for SMTHG

Accessible-luxury brands borrow the feeling of quality and craft, not the vocabulary. Specific, verifiable detail — fabric, construction, maker's name, piece count — signals elevation without switching into luxury-house words like "bespoke," "clients," or "the collection." That keeps the copy inside the direct, honest register Section 03's data rewards, while still reading as a step above mass market.

04 — Where borrowed slang backfires
Forced use of Gen Z slang, memes, or in-group language by a brand that does not own that culture is a repeated, documented failure mode — it reads as try-hard rather than relatable, and can misfire badly when the slang originates in a specific subculture the brand has no connection to. Source: Zinzin, "Brands Don't Have the Rizz: Gen Z Slang Is Falling Flat in Marketing"

The counter-example is Duolingo, whose "unhinged" owl persona works specifically because one dedicated team member owns the voice, the character is narrow and consistent (obsessed with language learning, "threatening but not mean"), and the tone was built from real user memes rather than copied from them. Duolingo's own brand guidelines document the tone deliberately, rather than leaving it to improvisation. Source: Duolingo Brand Guidelines — Tone.

Practical read for SMTHG

A meme-driven, slang-heavy voice is only defensible with a named owner and daily attention — it is a full-time craft, not a copy style anyone can apply occasionally. Without that ownership, the safer default per the data in Section 03 is plain, direct, consistent language.

05 — US and Europe are not the same audience
US and UK Gen Z skew individualist — self-expression is prioritized in messaging. Ecological/circular framing lands harder in Europe than in the US, and European Gen Z women show a stronger pull toward solitary creative pursuits (writing, sewing) versus the US skew toward social/gaming leisure. Sources: Comtec Translations, "Talking to Gen Z: An International Guide", 15 Jan 2025 · Ipsos, "Exploring the nuances: How Gen Z females in the USA and Europe differ", 3 Jun 2023

06 — Reference brand voices in adjacent categories
Fashion resale and community platforms already serving this audience split into two working patterns: one collective, simple voice built around a single imperative (Vinted), or several first-person voices that carry the platform's stylishness through the community itself rather than through brand copy (Depop). Both are viable; neither leans on borrowed slang from the brand's own mouth.

07 — Checking a reference campaign against its source
H&M's "There are no rules in fashion" line is from the 2015 "Close the Loop" campaign. The full line is "There are no rules in fashion but one: recycle your clothes" — the "no rules" opening is a setup for a garment-recycling call to action, not a freestanding self-expression thesis. Source: H&M Group, "Close the Loop" campaign page, 3 Sep 2015 · LBBOnline campaign coverage

The film's imagery — diverse casting, self-expression through clothing — is genuine and reusable as a mood reference. But if "no rules" language is used in SMTHG copy, it should carry its own payoff (a specific action or feature), the way H&M's line paid off into a recycling instruction, rather than standing alone as an abstract identity claim — which Section 03's data flags as the lower-priority register for this audience.
