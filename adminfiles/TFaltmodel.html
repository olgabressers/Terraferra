01Three Concept Blocks
01 · Reverse Marketplace
Designer posts a brief. Influencers apply. AI ranks the matches.
Today a small brand finds influencers one at a time — emails, DMs, weeks of back-and-forth for a handful of replies. Flip the direction. The designer posts the brief once — what kind of drop, what audience, what commission — and influencers apply to it. The system ranks applicants by how well their existing content matches the brief.
How it works
  • Designer posts a brief: drop aesthetic, audience, commission
  • Influencers browse open briefs and apply with one tap
  • AI ranks applicants by how visually similar their content is to the brief
  • Designer accepts the top matches
02 · Visual Style Profile
Each user gets a style profile from 5 photos.
Most apps recommend based on what the user clicks or buys. Proposed alt-model: at sign-up, each user uploads 5 outfits they like. The AI builds a style profile. The same profile then ranks what shows up in the feed, what drop alerts the user gets and — for influencers — which briefs they match.
How it works
  • At sign-up the user uploads 5 outfit photos
  • AI turns them into a style profile
  • The same profile is used everywhere: feed, alerts, brief matching
  • Instagram does not let third parties read likes / saves silently — the 5-photo upload is the workaround
03 · Social Discovery × AI
Recommendations use three signals at once, not one.
Most apps use one signal: tags (Farfetch), follows (ShopMy), or clicks (Amazon). Proposed alt-model: TF combines all three. The user's feed ranks every drop on style fit, social fit and how fast the drop is selling — at the same time. The combination was not observed in the platforms surveyed (see Section 03 discovery-signal comparison).
How it works
  • Style fit: how close the drop is to the user's style profile
  • Social fit: how many people the user follows are buying or voting
  • Drop momentum: pre-order rate + co-creation vote count
  • Every drop in the feed ranks on all three at the same time
Problem Emerging fashion designers need to find their audience, the right influencers and paying consumers. Existing platforms each solve one piece. Farfetch and SSENSE sell catalogs (need inventory + reputation). LTK and ShopMy attach creator links (need an existing audience). Kickstarter funds projects (no commerce flow). No single platform closes the loop for emerging fashion.

B · Reverse Marketplace · phasing
From emerging designers to large fashion houses — fashion-vertical only
Year 1 · Own the niche
Emerging designers
Deals at $1–5K per influencer drop. Pearpop and TRIBE publicly serve Fortune-500 brands (Microsoft, Netflix, Mars, Unilever) — this segment is not their stated target.
Year 2–3 · Mid-market
Fashion-vertical brands
H&M, Mango, Uniqlo, COS, & Other Stories, Arket, Ganni. Higher per-deal value. Requires the influencer network from Year 1.
Year 4+ · Optional
Enterprise fashion
LVMH groups, Kering, Inditex — only if team and capital support a separate enterprise sales motion.
02Discovery signals — Terra Ferra vs the rest
What signals each platform uses to surface products.
Platform Visual style signal Social signal Drop / momentum signal Net effect
Farfetch / Mytheresa No catalog browse, tags, text search No No Inventory catalog with editorial curation. No personalisation based on social or community signal.
LTK / ShopMy No Yes follow graph (who the user follows) No Affiliate links attached to creators the user already follows. Discovery limited to existing creator network.
Rednote · TikTok Yes visual content algorithmic feed Yes implicit (engagement signal) No generic algorithmic, not drop-aware Best-in-class visual + behavioural feed but generalist. No drop / pre-order / commerce-native ranking.
Terra Ferra · proposed Yes 5-photo style profile vector Yes explicit follow graph Yes co-creation vote rate + pre-order rate Three signals at once. Each drop in the feed ranks on style fit × social fit × drop momentum simultaneously.
The behavioural difference Farfetch shopper finds product through search and editorial. Rednote user finds product through algorithmic feed but with no buying loop. The proposed TF flow combines all three: drops a user's friends are pre-ordering, ranked by their style profile, with the option to vote on the design before production. Closer to social commerce than to a catalog marketplace.
Positioning vs Existing Categories
Catalog marketplaces (Farfetch, Wolf & Badger, Not On The High Street) sell inventory through search and browse. Affiliate networks (LTK, ShopMy) attach creator links to existing products. Creator-marketing platforms (Pearpop, TRIBE, Cohley) run campaigns for Fortune-500 brands at $50K+ budgets. None combine social discovery, visual AI and a reverse-marketplace structure inside one product, positioned for emerging affordable-luxury designers at $1K–$5K per influencer deal.

04Instagram Style Scan — Options & Constraints
Key constraint No fully invisible legal path exists for personal Instagram accounts. Meta's APIs do not let third-party apps read a user's likes, saves or follows. Personal accounts — the majority of users — cannot expose any post content via API. The closest compliant path is OAuth + 5-photo upload at ≈ 30 seconds onboarding. Five compliant options below; A + B combined is the recommended pattern.
Option How it works Coverage UX time Risk / status
A · Instagram Login + manual upload Source: developers.facebook.com OAuth captures identity (username, profile photo). User then uploads 5 outfit photos. FashionCLIP builds the style vector from the uploads. 100% of users ≈ 30 seconds Low risk · Meta-sanctioned
B · Business / Creator API auto-pull Source: developers.facebook.com OAuth + Instagram Graph API reads the user's recent media (own posts only). Style vector auto-built. No likes / saves / follows accessible. ≈ 5–10% of users (Business + Creator accounts only) ≈ 5 seconds Low risk · Meta-sanctioned · app-review required
C · Public profile URL scrape User pastes their public Instagram profile URL. TF scrapes recent posts via headless browser or 3rd-party data provider. ≈ 90% of users (public accounts) ≈ 10 seconds High risk · Meta TOS violation · account & IP bans · prior litigation precedent
D · iOS / Android share extension User shares 5 Instagram posts to the TF mobile app via the OS share sheet. TF reads the shared image data directly. Mobile app users only ≈ 30 seconds (multiple shares) Low risk · user-initiated
E · Browser extension Chrome / Safari extension captures images as the user browses Instagram. Style vector built passively. Desktop users with extension installed Passive (over days) Medium risk · extension-store review · privacy positioning
Recommended pattern Combine A + B in a single flow. OAuth login first. If the connected account is Business or Creator, pull recent media via API and build the style vector in 5 seconds. If the account is Personal (the majority), prompt the user to upload 5 photos — same 30-second onboarding. D is an add-on for the iOS / Android apps. C is excluded on TOS grounds.
